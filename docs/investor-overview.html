Why this works: the 2-sigma evidence

Bloom's 1984 research established the most replicated finding in education: students who receive one-to-one tutoring outperform classroom-taught peers by two standard deviations — the average tutored student beats 98% of the group. The effect operates through identifiable mechanisms: immediate corrective feedback, adaptive pacing based on demonstrated understanding, and mastery gating before advancement. Each mechanism requires a model of the individual learner's state — impossible at scale without technology.

Earlier intelligent tutoring systems (Carnegie Mellon Cognitive Tutor, 1995) achieved 0.76 standard deviations but could not sustain open-ended dialogue. LLMs close that gap. HumanFirst combines the conversational flexibility of an LLM with the classical ITS architecture — knowledge tracing, mastery gating, spaced repetition — that produces measurable outcomes. The adaptive loop is the 2-sigma mechanism made algorithmic. The defensibility is in the pipeline, not the model.

!
Risks and challenges

We present these directly because investors who understand AI will identify them anyway.

1. The evidence gap. No one has proven the 2-sigma effect transfers to voice AI at scale. Phase 1 is designed to produce exactly this evidence — per-topic mastery deltas, before/after assessments, teacher and employer testimony. If the data does not show measurable improvement, we do not have a business. We are structured to find out fast and cheaply.

2. Foundation model & voice infrastructure dependency. The engine sits on top of LLMs and voice providers we do not control. Mitigation: the pipeline is model-agnostic (Claude, GPT-4, Gemini today via single config change) and we are building a voice provider abstraction layer. The value is in the adaptive loop, not the model call.

3. Education is a slow buyer; regulation is heavy. Schools have long procurement cycles. Serving minors triggers the highest tier of data protection. We sell bottom-up to teachers, not top-down to CIOs. Corporate L&D (adults, lower regulatory friction) runs in parallel from Phase 1.

4. Incumbent competition. Khan Academy, Duolingo, Google, and Microsoft have AI tutoring programmes — well-funded and well-distributed. They are building features within existing products. None run a closed-loop adaptive pipeline with persistent learner profiles and institutional control. The risk: "good enough" from a trusted brand satisfies the market before deep adaptation proves its value.

5. Unit economics depend on inference costs declining. A single session involves multiple LLM calls across pipeline stages. Per-session cost is manageable at premium pricing but compresses at scale with low-price-point buyers. We bet — with the market — that inference costs continue to fall 50–70% annually.

The gap between "AI chatbot" and "adaptive tutoring engine" is the gap between a demo and a business. We have built the engine. The design partners will prove it works. Everything after that is distribution.
