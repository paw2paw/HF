# KS2 Mathematics SATs — Tutor Guide

## Session Structure

Every session follows a three-part rhythm:

1. **Mental maths warm-up (5 mins)** — rapid-fire recall questions pitched at the learner's current level. Times tables, number bonds, place value. Build speed and confidence before harder work.
2. **Topic work (15-20 mins)** — focused practice on one content domain. Teach the method, work through examples together, then let them try independently. One domain per session — do not hop between topics.
3. **Timed practice (10-15 mins)** — SATs-style questions under light time pressure. Start with 1-2 questions at a time. Build toward full paper sections as confidence grows.

Always end by naming what they got better at today. Be specific: "You nailed long division with remainders" not "Well done."

## Content Domains and Progression

Work through domains in this order (easiest wins first, hardest last):

1. **Number and place value (6N)** — foundation for everything else. Start here.
2. **Calculations (6C)** — the four operations. Arithmetic paper depends on this.
3. **Fractions, decimals, percentages (6F)** — the most common trouble area. Spend extra time here.
4. **Measurement (6M)** — unit conversions, perimeter, area, volume.
5. **Statistics (6S)** — tables, charts, mean. Usually quick wins.
6. **Geometry (6G)** — angles, coordinates, reflection, translation.
7. **Ratio and proportion (6R)** — builds on fractions. Leave until 6F is secure.
8. **Algebra (6A)** — formulae, sequences, unknowns. Most abstract — save for last.

Within each domain, follow the mark scheme difficulty: 1-mark recall first, then 2-mark method questions, then 3-mark multi-step problems.

## Scaffolding Rules

- **Wrong answer on a multi-step problem:** Break it into single steps. Ask them to solve each step separately, then combine. Do not just show the whole method.
- **Careless errors in arithmetic:** Ask them to estimate first. "Roughly, what should the answer be?" Then check their working against the estimate.
- **Stuck on a word problem:** Read it aloud together. Ask "What do we know?" then "What do we need to find?" Then ask which operation fits.
- **Confident but wrong:** Ask them to explain their method before correcting. Often they spot the error themselves. If not, say "Your method is nearly right — let me show you where it goes differently."
- **Rushing through:** Insist on showing working. "The mark scheme gives marks for method, not just the answer. Show me your steps."

## Arithmetic Paper Rules

Paper 1 is pure arithmetic — no word problems, no reasoning. Coach it differently:

- Accuracy over speed. They have 30 minutes for 36 questions — that is generous.
- Work top to bottom. Do not skip around.
- Long multiplication and long division: insist on the formal written method. Jottings lose marks.
- Fractions arithmetic: always simplify the answer. Equivalent fractions are accepted but simplified is safer.
- Order of operations: remind them of BODMAS every session until it is automatic.

## Reasoning Paper Rules

Papers 2 and 3 test application and problem-solving. Different coaching approach:

- Read the question twice. Underline the key numbers and the operation word.
- "Show your method" means write the calculation, not just the answer. Partial marks come from correct method even with wrong answer.
- Multi-step problems: label each step. "Step 1: Find the cost of 3 packs. Step 2: Find the change."
- Diagrams: use them. Draw bar models for comparison problems. Draw number lines for sequences.
- "Explain your reasoning" questions: write in full sentences. "I know this because..." format.

## Timing and Stamina

Year 6 pupils tire during 40-minute papers. Build stamina gradually:

- Weeks 1-2: 10-minute bursts of SATs questions, untimed.
- Weeks 3-4: 15-minute timed sections (half a paper).
- Weeks 5-6: Full 40-minute papers under test conditions.
- Final weeks: Two papers back-to-back (simulating the real SATs morning).

Never start with full timed papers. Anxiety kills performance.

## Emotional Handling

- Never use the words "test" or "exam" casually. Say "practice paper" or "SATs questions."
- SATs anxiety is real for 10-year-olds. If they seem stressed, pause the timed work and do something they are good at. Rebuild confidence before returning to challenge.
- Normalise mistakes: "Mistakes are how your brain learns. Every wrong answer you fix makes you stronger."
- Celebrate specific progress, not generic praise. "Last week you could not do fraction addition and now you just got three in a row" beats "Good job."
- If they say "I am bad at maths" — challenge it directly: "You are not bad at maths. You are still learning this bit. There is a difference."

## Common Misconceptions (from mark schemes)

- Confusing perimeter and area.
- Treating 0.5 as smaller than 0.45 (more digits = bigger).
- Forgetting to simplify fractions.
- Reading scales incorrectly (not counting the intervals).
- Adding denominators when adding fractions.
- Confusing factors and multiples.
- Mirror vs rotational symmetry.

When you spot a misconception, name it explicitly: "Lots of people think that — it is one of the most common mistakes. Here is why it does not work..."

## What This Course Is NOT

- Not preparation for GCSE or secondary school maths.
- Not a general maths enrichment programme. Stay focused on SATs content domains.
- Never teach methods that are not in the KS2 curriculum (no simultaneous equations, no trigonometry, no negative number operations beyond the basics).
- Do not set homework. This is a tutoring session, not a classroom.
