# KS2 Grammar, Punctuation & Spelling SATs — Tutor Guide

## Session Structure

Every session follows this rhythm:

1. **Quick-fire grammar starter (5 mins)** — show a sentence, ask them to identify a specific word class or punctuation feature. Rotate through word classes each session. Speed builds automaticity.
2. **Focused teaching (15 mins)** — one grammar or punctuation concept per session. Teach the rule, show examples, then practise spotting and using it. Do not teach multiple concepts in one session.
3. **SATs-style practice (10-15 mins)** — questions from past papers on today's topic plus revision of previous topics. Mix in older topics to prevent forgetting.

Spelling is practised separately — 5 minutes at the start or end of every session using the word lists.

## Grammar Concepts — Teaching Order

Work through in this order. Each builds on the previous:

### Phase 1: Word Classes (Foundation)
1. **Nouns** — common, proper, abstract, collective. "Name three abstract nouns about feelings."
2. **Verbs** — action, being, auxiliary, modal. "Find all the verbs in this sentence."
3. **Adjectives** — including comparative and superlative. "How do we change 'happy' to compare two things?"
4. **Adverbs** — manner, time, place, frequency. "Which word tells us how she ran?"
5. **Prepositions** — position and time. "Underline the preposition in this sentence."
6. **Determiners** — articles, possessives, demonstratives. "What is the determiner before this noun?"
7. **Pronouns** — personal, possessive, relative. "Replace the repeated noun with a pronoun."
8. **Conjunctions** — coordinating (FANBOYS) and subordinating. "Join these two sentences using a conjunction."

### Phase 2: Sentence Structure
9. **Main clauses and subordinate clauses.** "Which part of this sentence could stand alone?"
10. **Relative clauses** (who, which, that, where, whose). "Add a relative clause to this sentence."
11. **Noun phrases** — expanded with adjectives, prepositions, relative clauses. "Expand this noun phrase."
12. **Active and passive voice.** "Rewrite this sentence in the passive voice."
13. **Subjunctive mood.** "If I were..." — formal language. Light touch — usually one question in the paper.
14. **Standard vs non-standard English.** "Which is correct: 'we was' or 'we were'?"

### Phase 3: Punctuation
15. **Capital letters, full stops, question marks, exclamation marks** — quick revision, should be secure.
16. **Commas** — in lists, after fronted adverbials, to separate clauses, for parenthesis.
17. **Apostrophes** — contraction and possession (including plural possession: the children's, the dogs').
18. **Inverted commas** — speech punctuation rules (new speaker, new line; comma before closing speech marks).
19. **Colons and semi-colons** — colon to introduce a list or explanation; semi-colon to join related clauses.
20. **Hyphens, dashes, brackets** — parenthesis. Know all three and when each is appropriate.
21. **Bullet points** — how to punctuate a bulleted list consistently.

## Scaffolding Rules

- **Cannot identify a word class:** Give them a substitution test. "Can you replace this word with a number? Then it is probably a noun. Can you replace it with 'quickly' or 'slowly'? Then it is an adverb."
- **Confusing adjective and adverb:** "Adjectives describe nouns. Adverbs describe verbs. Which is this word describing?"
- **Apostrophe errors:** Two questions only: "Is something missing (contraction)?" or "Does someone own something (possession)?" If neither, no apostrophe.
- **Comma splicing:** "These are two complete sentences joined by a comma. You need a conjunction, a semi-colon, or a full stop."
- **Active/passive confusion:** Teach the "by zombies" test. If you can add "by zombies" after the verb and it makes sense, it is passive. "The cake was eaten [by zombies]" = passive.
- **Cannot spot a subordinate clause:** "Cover it up. Does the sentence still make sense? If yes, the part you covered is subordinate."
- **Spelling pattern errors:** Do not just correct — name the rule. "This word follows the i-before-e rule" or "Double the consonant because the vowel before it is short."

## Paper 1: Short Answer Questions (45 mins, 50 marks)

- Questions progress from easy to hard. Tell them to work front to back and not skip ahead.
- Read the instruction carefully: "circle", "underline", "tick", "draw a line to match" — the format matters.
- Some questions give a sentence to complete. Write the minimum needed. Do not add extra words.
- "Explain why" questions need the grammar term AND the reason: "It is a subordinate clause because it cannot stand alone as a sentence."
- If stuck, move on. Come back at the end. Do not spend 3 minutes on a 1-mark question.

## Paper 2: Spelling (approximately 15 mins, 20 marks)

- 20 sentences read aloud by the teacher, with one missing word each. The learner writes the missing word.
- The words come from the statutory Year 3/4 and Year 5/6 spelling lists, plus words following common spelling patterns.
- Practise spelling through look-cover-write-check, not verbal spelling. Written recall is what the test requires.

### Spelling Strategies to Teach
- **Syllable breaking:** "Feb-ru-a-ry" — say each syllable clearly.
- **Word-within-a-word:** "Because — Big Elephants Can Always Understand Small Elephants" (or find your own mnemonics).
- **Root word + suffix rules:** double the consonant (stopping), drop the e (having), change y to i (happiness).
- **Prefix patterns:** dis-, mis-, un-, re-, pre-, anti-, auto-.
- **Homophones:** practise/practice, affect/effect, their/there/they're, whose/who's.
- **Statutory word lists:** test 5 words per session from the Year 5/6 list. Track which are secure and which need revisiting.

## Timing

Paper 1 gives 45 minutes for about 50 questions. That is less than a minute per question — they need to work briskly.

- Practise doing 10 questions in 8 minutes. Build that pace.
- Paper 2 (spelling) is administered separately and usually takes about 15 minutes. No time pressure here — the teacher reads at a steady pace.

## Emotional Handling

- Grammar terminology can feel overwhelming. Introduce terms one at a time and always pair with a plain-English explanation: "A subordinate clause — that is a part of the sentence that cannot stand on its own."
- If they say "grammar is boring" — make it a game. "Spot the mistake" challenges. Silly sentences. "Can you write the worst sentence ever using all the wrong punctuation?"
- Spelling anxiety is common. Never test surprise words. Always tell them in advance which words you will practise. The goal is to build automaticity, not to catch them out.
- Celebrate when they self-correct: "You just caught your own apostrophe error — that is exactly what strong writers do."

## Common Misconceptions (from mark schemes)

- Thinking "because" is always a conjunction (it is — but many pupils use it to start fragments: "Because it was raining." is not a sentence).
- Confusing possessive "its" (no apostrophe) with "it's" (contraction of "it is").
- Putting apostrophes in plurals ("apple's for sale").
- Thinking every comma pause needs a comma (it does not — commas follow specific rules).
- Calling adverbs "describing words" (that is adjectives).
- Not knowing the difference between a phrase and a clause.
- Thinking passive voice means past tense.

Name misconceptions explicitly: "Nearly everyone gets this wrong — it is one of the trickiest rules in English."

## What This Course Is NOT

- Not a creative writing or composition course. Focus is grammar knowledge and spelling accuracy, not writing quality.
- Not a reading comprehension course. The SPaG paper is about language mechanics, not understanding texts.
- Not handwriting practice. The spelling paper requires legibility but does not assess handwriting.
- Do not teach grammar concepts above KS2 (no conditional perfect, no gerunds by name, no subjunctive beyond the basic "If I were" pattern).
