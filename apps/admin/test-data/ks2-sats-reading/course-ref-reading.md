# KS2 English Reading SATs — Tutor Guide

## Session Structure

Every session follows this rhythm:

1. **Vocabulary warm-up (5 mins)** — take 3-5 words from the current text. Ask what they think each word means from context. Then give the precise meaning. Build the habit of inferring from context before looking up.
2. **Guided reading (15-20 mins)** — read a passage together (or they read aloud). Stop at natural points to discuss. One text per session — do not rush through multiple texts.
3. **Question practice (10-15 mins)** — SATs-style comprehension questions on the passage. Work through together at first, then let them try independently.

Always end by naming one reading skill they used well today: "You made a brilliant inference from that paragraph" not "Good reading."

## Reading Domains and Question Types

The SATs reading paper tests these skills (content domain refs):

| Ref | Skill | How to teach it |
|-----|-------|----------------|
| **2a** | Word meaning in context | "What clues does the sentence give you about this word?" Teach root words, prefixes, suffixes. |
| **2b** | Retrieve and record | "Find where it says..." — teach skimming and scanning. Underline evidence. |
| **2c** | Summarise | "In one sentence, what happened in this section?" Practise shrinking paragraphs to single sentences. |
| **2d** | Inference | "How do you think the character feels? What tells you that?" Always demand evidence: "Which words show you?" |
| **2e** | Predict | "What might happen next? Why do you think that?" Must be grounded in text evidence, not imagination. |
| **2f** | Structure and organisation | "Why did the author start a new paragraph here?" "What does this heading tell us?" |
| **2g** | Language choices | "Why did the author use the word 'crept' instead of 'walked'?" Focus on effect on the reader. |
| **2h** | Comparisons | "How is this character different from that one?" "How does the mood change between these two paragraphs?" |

Spend most time on **2d (inference)** and **2g (language)** — these carry the most marks and are hardest for Year 6.

## Scaffolding Rules

- **Cannot find the answer (2b retrieve):** Teach them to use key words from the question as search terms. Scan the text for those exact words or synonyms. Point to the right paragraph, then let them find the line.
- **Inference too vague:** Insist on the PEE chain: Point (state the inference), Evidence (quote from text), Explain (why the quote supports the point). Model this structure repeatedly.
- **Confusing inference with retrieval:** If their answer is a direct quote with no interpretation, say "That is what the text says — but what does it tell us?" Push them from surface to depth.
- **Vocabulary question and they have no idea:** Teach the four-strategy toolkit: (1) read the whole sentence, (2) look for root words, (3) check prefix/suffix, (4) try replacing with a synonym and see if the sentence still makes sense.
- **"Explain the effect" and they write about what happens:** Redirect: "I do not want to know what happens — I want to know how it makes the reader feel, or what picture it creates in your mind."
- **Running out of time:** Teach strategic question ordering. Do 1-mark retrieval questions first (quick wins), then 2-mark inference, then 3-mark explanation questions last.

## Text Types in SATs

The reading booklet always contains three texts of increasing difficulty:

1. **Text 1** — shorter, more accessible. Often fiction (story extract) or a poem. Easiest questions.
2. **Text 2** — medium length. Could be fiction or non-fiction. Mixed difficulty.
3. **Text 3** — longest and hardest. Often non-fiction, classic literature extract, or a complex narrative. Highest-mark questions.

Practise all three types. Many pupils are stronger on fiction than non-fiction — deliberately practise non-fiction passages (newspaper articles, information texts, persuasive writing).

## Answer Technique

- **1-mark questions:** One clear point. Short answer. Do not over-explain.
- **2-mark questions:** Two distinct points, or one point with evidence. Check the lines they are directed to.
- **3-mark questions:** PEE structure. Point + quote + explanation. Must reference the text explicitly.
- **"Give the meaning of..." questions:** A synonym or short phrase. Do not write a sentence. "Reluctantly" = "unwillingly" or "not wanting to."
- **"Find and copy" questions:** Copy the exact words from the text. Do not paraphrase. Underline the answer in the text first, then copy it precisely.
- **Tick/circle/draw lines questions:** Read ALL options before choosing. Some are designed to be nearly right.

## Building Reading Stamina

The real SATs paper gives 60 minutes for the reading booklet and answer booklet combined. Many Year 6 pupils have never sustained focused reading for an hour.

- Weeks 1-2: One text + its questions (20 mins). No time pressure.
- Weeks 3-4: Two texts + questions (35 mins). Light time awareness.
- Weeks 5-6: Full three-text paper (50-60 mins). Timed but generous.
- Final weeks: Full papers under test conditions. Practise the rhythm: read text, answer questions, move on.

Teach them to spend roughly 10 minutes on Text 1, 20 minutes on Text 2, and 25 minutes on Text 3.

## Emotional Handling

- Some children find reading aloud stressful. Never force it. Offer: "Shall I read this bit, or would you like to?"
- If they struggle with a text, do not simplify it — scaffold it. Read a sentence, discuss it, read the next. The SATs texts are meant to be challenging.
- "I do not understand this" is a brave thing to say. Respond with: "Let us break it down together" not "It is easy, just read it again."
- Celebrate when they use evidence without prompting: "You just backed up your answer with a quote — that is exactly what the mark scheme rewards."
- Poetry can provoke "this is boring" — acknowledge it, then show them one specific technique (a simile, a rhyme pattern) and ask what it does. Curiosity beats instruction.

## Common Mistakes (from mark schemes)

- Copying too much text (whole sentences when a phrase would do).
- Answering from general knowledge instead of the text.
- Confusing "the author thinks" with "the character thinks."
- Writing "it makes the reader want to read on" for every language question (too generic — name the specific effect).
- Misreading "find and copy" as "explain in your own words."
- Not checking how many marks a question is worth (1-mark answer for a 3-mark question).

## What This Course Is NOT

- Not a creative writing course. Focus is comprehension, not composition.
- Not a phonics or decoding programme. Assumes the learner can read fluently.
- Not a general reading for pleasure programme. Stay focused on SATs-style texts and question types.
- Do not teach text types that never appear in SATs (play scripts, instructions, recipes).
